# Story and layouts

## Choose a narrative shape

### Decision or proposal deck

1. Decision and recommendation
2. Why now
3. Evidence or diagnosis
4. Proposed approach
5. Impact and tradeoffs
6. Execution plan
7. Decision required

### Pitch deck

1. Vision or opening tension
2. Problem
3. Insight
4. Solution
5. Product proof
6. Market and timing
7. Business model or value creation
8. Traction/evidence
9. Go-to-market
10. Team
11. Ask and destination

### Research or report deck

1. Executive answer
2. Scope and method
3. Key findings
4. Evidence sequence
5. Implications
6. Recommendations
7. Limitations and appendix

### Keynote or thought-leadership deck

1. Provocation
2. Shared reality
3. Reframe
4. Memorable evidence sequence
5. New model
6. Practical consequences
7. Closing image or call to action

Do not force every requested deck into these exact orders. Preserve the underlying logic: establish stakes, prove the claim, resolve the tension, and end with a clear action or synthesis.

## Match layout to purpose

### Statement

Use a short sentence, large type, and one supporting image or number. Suitable for opening, transition, or conclusion.

### Hero evidence

Use one dominant metric or chart with a concise interpretation. Put methodology and source in a quiet footer.

### Comparison

Use two aligned fields with the same visual grammar. Compare on explicit dimensions; avoid two unrelated card piles.

### Sequence or process

Use a directional line, numbered stages, or progressive frames. Keep step descriptions parallel and brief.

### System or architecture

Use a small number of meaningful layers or nodes and explicit relationships. Avoid decorative spaghetti diagrams.

### Portfolio or examples

Use a consistent image grid with strong crops and minimal captions. Let the work dominate.

### Quote or testimony

Use one excerpt, attribution, and contextual proof. Do not enlarge an unverified quote merely for drama.

### Table

Use only when the audience must compare exact values. Highlight the row or column that supports the conclusion and move exhaustive detail to an appendix.

## Rhythm

Alternate information density and visual form. A useful sequence might be:

`statement → evidence → comparison → image → process → synthesis`

Avoid three consecutive slides with the same composition. Repetition is appropriate only for a deliberate series, such as three case studies with identical comparison logic.

## Editing test

For every slide, ask:

1. What must the audience remember?
2. What evidence earns that conclusion?
3. What can be spoken instead of displayed?
4. What is the simplest spatial arrangement that expresses the relationship?

Delete everything that does not survive these questions.
