# Design rules

Use these constraints as a quality floor. Adapt them to the audience, brand, language, and room size.

## Hierarchy

- Make the intended reading order obvious within two seconds.
- Use scale before color, color before decoration.
- Keep one element dominant. When everything is emphasized, nothing is.
- Write message titles as conclusions: “Retention rises after week three,” not “Retention analysis.”
- Keep titles short enough to remain visually dominant. Move qualifications into a subtitle or source note.

## Typography

- Start around 40–60 pt for presentation titles, 28–40 pt for section or message titles, 18–28 pt for body copy, and 11–16 pt for sources. Adjust for canvas size and viewing distance.
- For Chinese, use slightly larger body text and more line height than comparable Latin copy.
- Limit paragraphs to short statements. Convert sequences to steps; convert comparisons to spatial contrast.
- Use no more than two font families. Use weight, size, and spacing consistently.
- Avoid all caps for long text and avoid faux bold or faux italic CJK typography.

## Grid and spacing

- Establish consistent outer margins, typically 5–8% of slide width.
- Use a 4, 6, 8, or 12-column grid and keep major anchors aligned across slides.
- Base spacing on a small unit such as 8 px/pt and use deliberate multiples.
- Prefer asymmetric balance over mechanically centering every element.
- Let intentional whitespace separate ideas; do not box every group.

## Color

- Define background, primary text, secondary text, accent, divider, and semantic data colors before authoring.
- Maintain readable contrast. Never rely on subtle gray-on-gray for projected text.
- Use one accent sparingly for attention, progress, or key data.
- Do not introduce colors merely to make a slide feel less empty.
- Derive palettes from supplied brand evidence. Without brand evidence, choose a restrained domain-appropriate palette.

## Shapes and components

- Use borders, fills, or shadows only when they express grouping or depth.
- Prefer flat regions and whitespace to nested cards.
- Keep corner radii consistent. Use square or modest radii for editorial and executive work.
- Avoid UI chrome—pills, toggles, nav bars, browser frames—unless the slide is actually explaining a product interface.
- Use icons as labels or directional cues, not as decorative confetti.

## Images

- Use one strong image rather than a collage of weak images.
- Preserve aspect ratio and crop around the focal point.
- Use full-bleed images with intentional text-safe areas or place them on a disciplined grid.
- Match color temperature and treatment across a sequence.
- Do not use watermarked, low-resolution, or visibly synthetic filler images.

## Data graphics

- Start with the claim, then choose the simplest chart that proves it.
- Use bars for category comparison, lines for time, dots for precise comparison, and tables only when exact lookup matters.
- Remove redundant legends, gridlines, borders, and decimals.
- Highlight the evidence that supports the title; mute the rest.
- Keep scales honest, labels legible, and units explicit.

## Motion

- Use motion only to reveal sequence, causality, or focus.
- Prefer a few consistent transitions. Avoid effects that compete with the speaker.
- Ensure the static exported slide still communicates the full message.

## Anti-pattern audit

Reject or revise slides exhibiting:

- a title plus six equal cards;
- a centered heading over a long centered paragraph;
- decorative gradients or glows without brand justification;
- several unrelated visual metaphors;
- stock icons replacing concrete evidence;
- tiny labels used to force too much content onto one page;
- repeated identical layouts across most of the deck;
- a beautiful composition that no longer supports the stated conclusion.
